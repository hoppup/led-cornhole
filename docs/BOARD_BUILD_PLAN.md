# LED Cornhole Board — Premium Build Plan

**Date:** 2026-03-05  
**Goal:** Build premium regulation cornhole boards designed to house LED electronics  
**Standard:** ACL (American Cornhole League) regulation specs

---

## 1. Regulation Dimensions (ACL Spec)

| Spec | Measurement |
|------|-------------|
| **Playing surface** | 48" long × 24" wide |
| **Hole diameter** | 6" |
| **Hole center** | 9" from top edge, 12" from each side (centered) |
| **Front height** | 3–4" off the ground |
| **Back height** | 12" off the ground |
| **Surface material** | Wood (plywood), smooth |
| **Finish** | Semi-gloss or satin for proper bag slide |
| **Weight** | ~25–30 lbs per board (premium feel, won't slide during play) |
| **Board spacing** | 27 feet apart (front to front) during play |

| **1×4 Poplar boards** (frame) | 4 | 8' length | $24–32 ($6-8 ea) | Hardwood, dent-resistant, won't warp like pine. Cut to: 4× 48" + 4× 21" |

**Why these materials:**
- **3/4" Baltic Birch** — The gold standard. Pro builders (Cornhole Pro, YoreBoard, etc.) all use this. 1/2" is allowed by ACL rules but 3/4" is better: bags don't bounce, boards don't flex or warp, and you have thickness to route channels for wires on the underside.
- **Poplar frame** — Hardwood that's easy to work with. Stronger and more warp-resistant than pine or SPF. Pro builders use poplar for frames and legs.
- **Why NOT pine** — Pine warps, dents easily, and looks cheap. For premium boards, avoid it.

## 5. Build Steps

### Step 1: Cut the Tops
1. Rip the 4'×8' Baltic Birch sheet into **two 24" × 48" panels** using a table saw or circular saw with a straightedge guide
2. **Pro tip:** Use painter's tape along the cut line on the good side to prevent tear-out
3. Sand both faces with 150 grit, then 220 grit

### Step 2: Mark and Cut the Hole
1. Measure **9" from the top edge** and **12" from each side edge** — mark the center point
2. Draw a **6" diameter circle** (3" radius) using a compass
3. Apply painter's tape over the cut line (prevents tear-out)
4. Option A: Use a **6" hole saw** (cleanest cut)
5. Option B: Drill a starter hole inside the circle, then cut with a **jigsaw**
6. Sand the hole edge smooth — round it slightly with sandpaper or a router roundover bit

### Step 3: Route Wire Channels (LED Modification)
**This is the key step that makes these boards electronics-ready.**

On the **underside** of each top panel:
1. Mark channel paths from where LEDs will mount on the face to the electronics enclosure area
2. Using a router with a **3/8" straight bit, set to 1/4" depth**, route channels for wires:
   - Channel from scoreboard area → down to center of board (where ESP32 will mount)
   - Channel from hole ring area → to center
   - Channel from button locations on side rails → to center
   - Channel from edge LED strips → to center
3. Route a larger pocket (~3" × 4" × 1/4" deep) in the center for the ESP32 enclosure mounting area
4. **Don't go deeper than 1/4"** — you're working with 3/4" plywood, need to leave at least 1/2" of surface

**Drill pass-through holes:**
- Small holes (3/16") at each LED segment mounting location — wires pass from face through to underside channels
- One larger hole (1/2") near the side rail for button wires
- One hole near the hole for the ring LED wires

### Step 4: Build the Frame
1. Cut frame pieces:
   - 2× long rails at 48"
   - 2× short rails at 21" (fits inside the long rails — total frame = 48" × 24")
   - 1× center cross brace at 21" (placed ~16" from the back end for rigidity)
2. Drill pocket holes in the **ends of the short rails** and the **center brace**
3. Apply **Titebond III** to all joints
4. Assemble with **1-1/4" pocket hole screws**
5. Clamp and let dry 30 minutes minimum

### Step 5: Attach Top to Frame
1. Place the top panel face-down on a clean surface
2. Center the frame on the underside of the top
3. Apply glue along the top edges of the frame
4. Attach with pocket hole screws from inside the frame into the top — **every 6-8 inches along the long rails**
5. Alternatively: screw through the top into the frame with countersunk #8 screws and fill with wood filler (if you're wrapping/painting the top)

### Step 6: Build and Attach Legs
1. Cut 2 legs per board at **11-1/2" long**
2. Cut a **10° angle** on the top end (creates the proper board incline)
3. **Round the bottom edges** so legs pivot smoothly when folding
4. Position legs on the **inside of the back rail**, spaced about 3" in from each side
5. Drill a **1/4" hole** through the leg and frame rail
6. Install **carriage bolt + washer + wing nut** at each leg
7. Test: legs should swing freely and lock when tightened
8. When deployed, the back of the board should sit **12" off the ground**, front at **3-4"**

**Leg math:**
- Board top is 3/4" thick
- Frame rails are 3-1/2" tall (1×4 actual)
- Front of frame touches the ground: 3/4" + 3-1/2" = **4-1/4"** front height ✅ (ACL allows 3-4", this is close — sand the front frame edge slightly or use a 1×3 for the front rail to hit exactly 3.5")
- Legs at 11-1/2" with 10° angle: back height = ~**12"** ✅

### Step 7: Round Edges
1. Using a router with a **1/4" or 3/8" roundover bit**, round all exposed edges:
   - Top edges of the playing surface
   - Around the hole
   - Frame edges
   - Leg edges
2. This is a premium detail that makes the boards feel finished and professional
3. Sand all routed edges with 220 grit

### Step 8: Final Sanding
1. Sand the entire playing surface: **150 → 220 → 320 grit**
2. Sand frame and legs: **150 → 220 grit**
3. Wipe down with **tack cloth** to remove all dust
4. The surface should feel silky smooth

### Step 9: Finish (Apply Clear Coat)
**Using Varathane Water-Based Exterior Spar Urethane (recommended):**

1. Stir gently (don't shake — creates bubbles)
2. Apply **thin, even coats** with a foam brush using long strokes in one direction
3. **Playing surface: 5-7 coats**
   - Apply coat → dry 2 hours → light sand with 320 grit → tack cloth → repeat
   - Do NOT sand the final coat
4. **Frame and legs: 2-3 coats**
5. Wait **24 hours** after final coat before use
6. **Important:** Apply finish BEFORE mounting any electronics

**Why spar urethane over poly:**
- UV protection (won't yellow in sun)
- Flexible (handles outdoor temp changes without cracking)
- Water-based = fast dry, easy cleanup
- Self-leveling = smooth professional finish

### Step 10: Embed LEDs with Epoxy (Before Final Clear Coat)
**This is done AFTER sanding but BEFORE applying spar urethane.**

1. **Route LED channels on the face** of the board:
   - Use a router with a straight bit to cut channels ~4-5mm deep × ~12mm wide for each segment
   - Route a circular channel around the hole for the ring LEDs
   - Use a template or CNC for consistent, clean channels
   - Sand channel edges smooth

2. **Lay LED strip segments** into the channels:
   - Cut WS2812B strip into segments (4 LEDs each for scoreboard)
   - Solder thin wires between segments (following the 7-segment layout)
   - Route wires through the drilled pass-through holes to the underside
   - Hot glue or tape strips in place temporarily

3. **Test all LEDs** before sealing:
   - Connect to ESP32 on breadboard, verify every segment lights up
   - Fix any bad solder joints NOW — once epoxy is poured, there's no going back

4. **Place diffuser strips** over the LEDs:
   - Cut translucent white PETG or acrylic sheet into strips matching each channel
   - Place strips on top of the LEDs in each channel
   - The diffuser sits between the LEDs and the epoxy, spreading light evenly

5. **Pour clear epoxy:**
   - Mix table-top epoxy (TotalBoat or similar) in graduated cups — exact 1:1 ratio
   - Pour into channels in thin layers (1/8" max per pour)
   - Use a heat gun or torch to pop surface bubbles (quick pass, don't linger)
   - Let cure 24-48 hours between pours if multiple layers needed
   - Fill to slightly ABOVE the board surface (it'll sand down flush)

6. **Sand flush:**
   - After full cure (48-72 hours), sand the epoxy flush with the board surface
   - Progressive grit: 220 → 320 → 400 → 600
   - Wipe with tack cloth between grits
   - The surface should be perfectly smooth — you shouldn't feel any transition between wood and epoxy

### Step 11: Apply Final Clear Coat
Now apply spar urethane over the ENTIRE surface (wood + epoxy together):
1. Apply 5-7 coats of Varathane Water-Based Exterior Spar Urethane
2. Light sand with 320 grit between coats (not the last coat)
3. This seals everything into one unified, smooth playing surface

### Step 12: Mount Remaining Electronics
After the finish is fully cured (48-72 hours):
1. On the **underside**: connect all wires that were routed through the pass-through holes
2. Mount ESP32 enclosure on the underside (screws or velcro)
3. Install arcade buttons in the side rail (drill 30mm holes through rail)
4. Install hole ring LEDs (already embedded in epoxy on the face)
5. Connect all wiring to the ESP32
6. Mount power bank bracket with velcro on underside
7. Seal all wire pass-through holes with silicone from the underside
8. Final test of all electronics

### Button Placement
- Buttons mount on the **side rail** of the board (left or right side)
- Position: **about 2/3 down from the top** (easy reach when standing at the board)
- Drill holes through the rail matching your arcade button diameter (typically 30mm / ~1-3/16")
- Run button wires through the frame to the underside channel

### Hole Ring
- Route a **shallow channel (1/8" deep)** around the hole on the face of the board
- Or use a 3D printed ring channel that sits on top of the surface
- Epoxy or silicone the LED strip into the channel
- Clear cover/diffuser over the ring

## 8. Pro Tips

1. **Let the plywood acclimate** — store it flat in your shop for a few days before cutting. Prevents warping.
2. **Cut the good face up** on a table saw, **good face down** with a circular saw (blade rotation determines tear-out side)
3. **Use a sacrificial board** under the plywood when cutting the hole to prevent blow-out on the bottom
4. **Don't rush the finish** — each coat of spar urethane needs full dry time. Shortcuts = bubbles and peeling later.
5. **Pre-drill everything in poplar** — it's a hardwood and will split if you drive screws without pilot holes
6. **Route the wire channels BEFORE attaching the top to the frame** — way easier when the panel is flat on your bench
7. **Apply finish BEFORE electronics** — you don't want spar urethane on your LEDs
8. **Label your wires** — before routing them through channels, label each wire (masking tape + marker). You'll thank yourself during assembly.
9. **Test all electronics on a breadboard** before final installation — way easier to debug on a bench than inside a finished board
10. **Weight matters** — 3/4" Baltic Birch + poplar frame = ~28-30 lbs per board. Perfect. Heavy enough to stay put, not so heavy you can't carry them.
